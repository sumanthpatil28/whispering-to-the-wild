Chapter 8: The Shadow of Hunters

The rains had long since retreated, leaving the forest paths dappled with sunlight. Bhim noticed the animals were restless: Lila chirped uneasy notes, the monkeys moved through the trees more cautiously, and Jumbo’s great ears twitched at every sound.

He froze, staring at the cruel loop. Beside him, Kanti’s fur bristled. "A snare," she whispered. "Humans set it to catch legs and necks."

Before Bhim could speak, a plaintive bleat sounded deeper in the brush. Together they pushed through and found a young antelope struggling on the ground, its hind leg caught in another wire. Blood stained the leaves. Bhim’s throat tightened. "Hold still," he murmured, kneeling beside the trembling creature. His small fingers worked at the knot, and with a sharp twist the wire loosened. The antelope limped away, thanking him with a nod of its head before disappearing into the trees.

"Who would do this?" Bhim asked, anger and sadness swirling inside him.

Kanti’s eyes were hard. "Men who take lives for money. Poachers. They have come from the north. Their leader is called Rajan."

That evening Bhim sat with his parents by the fire and listened to the elders whisper about strangers camping near the river, men with nets and guns. His father frowned. "They are not like the company loggers," he said. "These men hunt what should not be hunted. The headman sent word to the ranger, but help is slow."

Bhim could not sleep. In the dark, Lila perched on his window ledge. "Tomorrow," she whispered, "we will find out more."

At dawn Bhim crept with his friends through the forest. They saw fresh boot prints, piles of meat salted and wrapped, and at last, through a veil of bamboo, they saw the poachers’ camp. Men sat around a smoky fire sharpening knives. Two cages held frightened monkeys. Near the fire stood a tall man with a scar across his cheek. He spoke in a low, commanding voice.

"We move deeper tomorrow," he said. "There is talk of a white tiger and elephants with tusks like curved swords. We take them all."

Kanti hissed softly. "That is Rajan," she said. "He hates the forest. He says animals are devils."

Bhim’s heart hammered. He wanted to burst from the bushes and shout, but Lila tugged at his hair. "Not yet," she whispered. "We must think."

They slipped away to the safety of the great banyan, where they called a meeting. Monkeys, deer, boars, birds, and even Kavi the old turtle gathered around Bhim as he spoke. "There are men with traps and guns," he said. "We must watch and warn each other. No one goes alone. If they come for you, cry out and we will help." The animals nodded. The forest, which had always felt like a playground, now felt like a battlefield. Bhim knew the Whisperers’ gift had called him for a reason. The fight was about to begin.

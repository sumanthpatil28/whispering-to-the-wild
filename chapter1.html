Chapter 1: The Beginning

Bhim lived at the edge of a small village where the fields met the forest. He loved to sit on the low stone wall that separated his family’s hut from the dirt road and listen to the birds chatter among themselves. Their songs were like stories, though for most people they were only sound. Since he was small, his mother would sit by his mat before he slept and tell him tales about their ancestors who could understand and speak with animals and trees. These legendary Whisperers, she said, heard the river’s sigh and the owl’s complaint and protected the forest. Bhim would drift off with visions of talking deer and murmuring banyan leaves curling through his head.

One night, after his mother had whispered such a story, Bhim dreamed that he was walking deep into a misty grove. In the dream he saw a ring of gentle figures bathed in moonlight. Though he did not know their faces, they felt like family. One bent down and placed a warm hand on his head. Another touched his cheek. As they blessed him, his surroundings blurred like leaves in the wind and he heard a single word: “Listen.” The warmth spread through him, then darkness came, and he slept again.

The next morning, as pale sunlight filtered through the thatch, Bhim sat on his usual spot on the wall. Every sound felt sharper, every rustle full of meaning. A little brown bird with a shimmering throat perched on the wall just a few handspans from his elbow, singing a clear, lilting tune. Her voice wove through the air like a question.

“You’re staring awfully hard,” said the whisper.

Bhim jumped, looking around for the speaker. He realized the words came from the bird. She blinked at him with bright black eyes. “Can you hear me?” she asked.

His mouth fell open. “I… I can hear you!” he gasped. His heart pounded as if he had been running. “Are you talking?”

The bird fluffed her feathers. “Of course I am talking. My name is Lila. I’ve been watching you for days. You listen better than the other children. Usually, only the squirrels listen to me, and they are terrible gossips.”

For a long moment Bhim could only stare. He had always felt there were meanings in the rustle of leaves and chirps of birds, but he had never expected them to arrange themselves into sentences. “Why can I understand you?” he whispered at last.

Lila cocked her head. “Maybe you have a gift. We have legends of humans who could listen. Maybe you are one of them.”

That evening, Bhim told his mother, speaking in a low voice so his father would not hear. She was kneading dough by the firelight, her bangles chiming softly with each movement. He told her about his dream of the shadowy figures who blessed him and the words of the bird. His mother’s hands stilled. She did not laugh or scold him. Instead she reached out and touched his cheek.

“I used to tell you about our ancestors who could whisper to the wild,” she said softly. “They could hear the thoughts of animals and trees. They were guardians. Perhaps the ones in your dream were those very ancestors. Perhaps they have blessed you with their gift.”

“Do you think I’m like them?” Bhim asked, both excited and afraid.

She smiled and pulled him close. “Maybe. The forest is part of our lives, Bhim. It feeds us and shelters us. To hear it is a blessing, but others may not understand. Keep this quiet for now. Listen carefully and be kind. The forest speaks in whispers.”

That night, Bhim lay awake on his mat, staring at the thatch above him. Outside, crickets sang and a breeze rustled the banana leaves. Every sound felt different now, weighted with possible meaning. He thought of Lila’s bright eyes, of his mother’s gentle stories, and of the mysterious figures who had touched him in his dream. Beyond the village, the forest waited. Bhim closed his eyes and dreamed of trees whose leaves murmured secrets and of a path leading deeper than he had ever gone.
